# Tim Rayner

> Software engineer. Builder of things that ship, not just things that work.

---

## On thinking in systems

I approach software the same way I'd approach a city plan — looking for the natural flow paths, where bottlenecks form, and what breaks first under stress. Good architecture isn't about predicting the future. It's about making the future cheaper to change.

When I sit with a problem long enough, I stop thinking about the solution and start thinking about the `shape` of the solution. That discipline tends to produce simpler code.

## On building

The best products I've worked on had one thing in common: the constraint was respected, not worked around. Tight deadlines, limited APIs, impossible briefs — these aren't blockers. They're the conditions under which interesting work gets made.

I default to shipping over polishing. Not because quality doesn't matter — it does, deeply — but because "done" teaches you things "perfect" never will.

## What I actually care about

- **Interfaces that disappear.** The best UI is the one the user doesn't notice.
- **Code that reads like prose.** If I have to explain what it does, it needs renaming.
- **The gap between working and right.** The second takes longer. It's worth it.
- **Opinionated, not attached.** Strong views, loosely held.

## How I collaborate

I like working with people who disagree well. The best technical decisions I've been part of came from someone saying `"no, actually—"` and having a reason.

I write things down. Thinking in prose forces a kind of clarity that whiteboarding rarely does.
